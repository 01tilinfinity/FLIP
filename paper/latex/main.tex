\begin{abstract}
Users often express information needs with negative constraints.
Beyond explicit negation, queries may ask for answers that include one concept while excluding another, or for entities that belong to a category but differ from a closely related instance.
Because the excluded concept still appears in the query text, dense retrievers may assign high similarity to documents about that concept even when the user asks to avoid it.
We introduce \method, a training-free reranking method for negation-sensitive retrieval.
\method uses a large language model to decompose a negative query with an exclusion constraint into a target intent and a flipped trap intent, then penalizes documents that are similar to the trap.
On ExcluIR \citep{zhang2024excluir}, \method reduces trap retrieval across four embedding models while preserving high answer recall under moderate penalty weights.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Users often express information needs by specifying not only what should be retrieved, but also what should be avoided.
A query may ask for documents about one topic while excluding a related entity, or for items that belong to a category but differ from a particular instance.
For example, ``documentaries about the Iraq war, excluding \textit{Gunner Palace}'' asks the retriever to preserve the broad topic while avoiding a specific, highly related title.
When such documents are used as evidence for generation, retrieving the excluded item can lead the system to ground its answer in material the user explicitly ruled out.

This setting is difficult for dense retrieval because the excluded concept is both salient and present in the query.
The retriever may assign high similarity to a document precisely because it matches the term the user asked to avoid.
Negative queries with exclusion constraints therefore require a distinction between inclusion and exclusion semantics that standard retrievers often do not encode reliably \citep{lee2026deotrainingfreedirectembedding}.

Recent benchmarks and systems study this weakness across minimally different negated pairs, realistic negative queries with exclusion constraints, and negative-constraint retrieval \citep{weller-etal-2024-nevir, zhang2024excluir, xu-etal-2025-negconstraint}.
However, benchmarks with document-level answer and trap links remain scarce, and many existing approaches require specialized training data or model changes.
We study whether an existing retriever can be made more faithful to the exclusion constraint in a negative query at inference time, without retraining it.

We propose \method, a knowledge-free flipped-intent penalty for negative queries with exclusion constraints.
Given such a negative query, a large language model produces two short rewrites: a target query that preserves what should be retrieved and a trap query that names the forbidden or flipped intent.
\method then penalizes documents that are similar to the trap intent.
The language model is used only for query rewriting; it does not inspect documents, score candidates, or use oracle answer and trap labels.

Our contributions are as follows.
\begin{itemize}
    \item We introduce a training-free method for negative queries with exclusion constraints. \method changes only inference-time scores and requires no task-specific supervision, fine-tuning, or retriever modification.
    \item We formulate a knowledge-free correction for fixed-corpus RAG settings. \method does not add external knowledge or rewrite the corpus; it changes the ranking induced by existing retriever scores.
    \item We evaluate the method on ExcluIR over the full retrieval corpus across four embedding models, and report a BoolQuestions-style contrastive evaluation in the appendix \citep{zhang2024excluir, zhang2024boolquestionsdoesdenseretrieval}.
    \item We analyze the recall--violation trade-off and identify failure cases where answer documents mention the excluded entity or lie close to the trap document in embedding space.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Our work builds on three strands of prior research.
The first studies negation and negative constraints as a persistent bottleneck for language understanding and retrieval.
The second develops embedding-level corrections for negation-aware retrieval.
The third uses rank fusion to combine retrieval signals, motivating our score-level reranking formulation.

\paragraph{Negative constraints in retrieval.}
Negation has long been a weak point for language understanding systems \citep{alhamoud2025visionlanguagemodelsunderstandnegation}.
Language models can be insensitive to negation, fail to capture lexical negation, and struggle to reason under negated statements \citep{truong-etal-2023-language}.
This weakness also appears in retrieval.
NevIR tests minimally different query pairs where negation reverses relevance \citep{weller-etal-2024-nevir}, while ExcluIR studies realistic negative queries with exclusion constraints where a retriever must return the target document without surfacing the excluded one \citep{zhang2024excluir}.
NegConstraint frames negative constraints as a logical consistency problem and applies neural-symbolic reranking \citep{xu-etal-2025-negconstraint}.
In biomedical QA, dense retrieval can blur support and contradiction when negation is central to the evidence \citep{sahoo2026negationsemanticdiagnosingdense}.

\paragraph{Embedding-level approaches to negation.}
A negated query still contains the entity or concept to be avoided, so dense encoders can place it near documents that violate the request.
DEO addresses this by decomposing positive and negative components and optimizing the query embedding at inference time for negation-aware retrieval \citep{lee2026deotrainingfreedirectembedding}.
\method keeps the retriever and document embeddings fixed, using rewrites only to expose target and trap intents before applying score-level penalties.

\paragraph{Rank fusion for reranking.}
Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) combines ranked lists with reciprocal-rank contributions rather than raw scores, making it useful when different retrieval systems produce incomparable score scales \citep{cormack2009reciprocal}.
This suggests an anti-RRF variant for exclusion constraints: keep the original-query rank signal but subtract the trap-query contribution, e.g., $1/(k_{\mathrm{rrf}}+r_q(d))-\beta/(k_{\mathrm{rrf}}+r_{\mathrm{trap}}(d))$.
However, rank-only penalties are sparse and coarse: because RRF ignores score magnitudes, semantically close target documents are frequently pushed out of the top-$k$ results.
\method keeps the anti-RRF intuition but implements the penalty with dense retrieval scores.

\section{K-FLIP}

\method is a retrieval-time reranking method for negative queries with exclusion constraints.
It operates on fixed retriever scores by decomposing the query into target and trap intents, then lowering the rank of documents that match the trap intent.
The method consists of two steps: query decomposition and score transformation.

\subsection{Query Decomposition}

Let $q$ be a negative query that contains an exclusion constraint.
\method uses GPT-4o mini as the decomposer and asks it to produce two rewrites:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $q_{\mathrm{target}}$: the positive information need, with the exclusion removed but the main topic preserved;
    \item $q_{\mathrm{trap}}$: the forbidden or flipped intent that should be penalized.
\end{itemize}
For example, the query ``location of Friday Harbor Airport without bringing up Dayton International Airport'' is decomposed into target intent ``location of Friday Harbor Airport'' and trap intent ``Dayton International Airport''.
The LLM is not used to score documents or inspect gold labels; it only produces these two strings.

\subsection{Score Transformation}

Let $s(d,q)$ be the retriever similarity score between document $d$ and query $q$.
The baseline ranks documents by $s(d,q)$, or by a cleaned rewrite of the original query when available.
\method uses dense similarity scores as a smoother penalty signal and computes
\begin{equation}
    S(d) = \alpha s(d,q) + \gamma s(d,q_{\mathrm{target}})
           - \beta s(d,q_{\mathrm{trap}}),
    \label{eq:kflip}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$, $\gamma$, and $\beta$ control the original-query, target, and trap terms.
The positive weights $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ select which retrieval signal to preserve: the original query score or the target-query score.
In our experiments, we do not tune $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ continuously.
We keep them as a discrete choice so that the positive evidence used for ranking remains fixed, and the effect of the trap penalty can be attributed to a single parameter.
Tuning $\alpha$, $\gamma$, and $\beta$ jointly would mix recall changes from positive-score reweighting with violation changes from trap demotion.
Instead, we evaluate two variants, baseline-minus-trap with $(\alpha,\gamma)=(1,0)$ and target-minus-trap with $(\alpha,\gamma)=(0,1)$.
The only tuned penalty weight is $\beta$, which controls how strongly documents similar to the trap query are demoted.
Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reports the baseline-minus-trap setting that preserves recall while stably reducing violation.
The appendix reports the full beta grid for both scoring variants, with $\beta=0$ serving as the zero-penalty reference for each variant.
Because all terms are retriever scores, \method can be applied to sparse or dense retrievers with comparable scoring functions.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Dataset.}
We evaluate on 3,452 negative queries with exclusion constraints from ExcluIR \citep{zhang2024excluir}.
Each example contains a query, a gold answer document, and a trap document corresponding to the excluded intent.
We score against the full ExcluIR corpus of 90,406 documents.
Gold and trap document indices are used only for evaluation, not for query decomposition, retrieval, or reranking.

\paragraph{Retrievers and Decomposition.}
We test four embedding models: OpenAI text-embedding-3-small, OpenAI text-embedding-3-large, Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B, and Qwen3-Embedding-4B.
For each query, we generate $q_{\mathrm{target}}$ and $q_{\mathrm{trap}}$ with GPT-4o mini.
For the main variant, we use the original query as the positive retrieval signal and $q_{\mathrm{trap}}$ only as a penalty signal: $S(d)=s(d,q)-\beta s(d,q_{\mathrm{trap}})$.
We evaluate $\beta \in [0,1]$ at intervals of 0.01 to characterize the recall--violation trade-off rather than to claim a deployment-ready tuned hyperparameter.
Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reports the setting with the lowest average violation among settings that preserve average recall over $k \in \{3,5,7,9\}$ relative to the baseline.
Appendix~\ref{tab:appendix-excluir-baseline-beta-000-049} reports the full beta grid, including the target-minus-trap variant.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Recall@$k$ measures whether the gold answer document appears in the top $k$.
Violation@$k$ measures whether the trap document appears in the top $k$.
For retrieval with exclusion constraints, higher recall and lower violation are better.

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} shows that \method reduces trap retrieval across all four retrievers.
The baseline retrievers achieve high recall, but they often retrieve the forbidden document as well: violation@5 ranges from 0.728 to 0.886.
The full beta grid in the appendix shows a clear recall--violation trade-off.
As $\beta$ increases, trap documents are demoted more strongly, but very large penalties can also demote answer documents that share evidence with the trap intent.
At $\beta=1.0$, average violation drops to 0.030--0.036 across retrievers, but average recall also falls to 0.779--0.835, well below the baseline range of 0.937--0.942.

Despite this trade-off, there are beta values where recall remains effectively unchanged while violation decreases.
At the representative settings in Table~\ref{tab:main-results}, average recall changes by at most 0.0002 from the baseline, while average violation decreases for every retriever.
The largest top-$k$ reduction occurs for text-embedding-3-small, where violation@5 decreases from 0.879 to 0.517 while recall@5 changes only from 0.938 to 0.936.

The appendix shows that the size of the violation reduction can vary by dataset, but the beta-dependent trade-off remains.
The range of recall-preserving beta values also differs by retriever, suggesting that the penalty strength should be selected based on the retriever and dataset.
Overall, the results show that a score-level trap penalty can preserve target-document retrieval while moving trap documents out of the top ranks.

\section{Failure Analysis}

We manually inspected failure cases for the trap-penalty variant.
Two patterns dominate.
First, many gold answer documents mention the excluded entity.
For example, an answer page about a film may list an actor that the query asks to exclude.
Because \method penalizes document-level similarity to the trap query, it can demote otherwise correct documents that contain incidental trap evidence.
Second, many answer and trap documents are near-neighbors in embedding space: they concern sibling entities, adaptations, cast members, or closely related institutions.
In these cases, the trap penalty often lowers both documents together.

These failures suggest that \method is a retrieval-stage correction, not a complete solution to negation.
Future systems should combine query-level flipped-intent penalties with passage- or span-level evidence checks so that an incidental mention of the forbidden entity does not automatically invalidate an otherwise useful document.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented \method, a knowledge-free flipped-intent penalty for negative queries with exclusion constraints.
By extracting a trap intent from the query and subtracting trap similarity, \method reduces forbidden-document retrieval across four dense retrievers while exposing a clear recall--violation trade-off.
Because it requires no task-specific training, \method can be easily integrated with existing dense retrievers as an inference-time correction.

\section*{Limitations}

\method depends on the quality of LLM-generated decompositions and assumes that retriever scores are comparable across target and trap queries.
The reported \method settings are selected on the evaluated sample to characterize the trade-off; future work should tune hyperparameters on a separate development set.
Our evaluation is limited to ExcluIR and dense retrieval because few benchmarks provide document-level annotations for both target and forbidden evidence.
Additional evaluations should include sparse retrieval, cross-encoder reranking, and downstream RAG answer quality.
The current linear penalty may over-demote correct documents that contain incidental mentions of excluded entities.

\section*{Ethical Considerations}

This work uses public benchmark data and only rewrites queries for retrieval; deployments should monitor over-penalization when excluded entities appear in otherwise relevant evidence.
