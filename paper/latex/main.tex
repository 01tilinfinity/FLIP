\begin{abstract}
Users often express information needs with negative constraints.
Beyond simple negation, queries frequently ask for answers that include one concept while excluding another, or for entities that belong to a category but differ from a closely related instance.
Such constraints are important for generating the answer the user actually wants, and failing to respect them can be harmful: in security-sensitive settings, highly ranking a document that should have been excluded may leak information into the final response.
Because the excluded concept still appears in the query text, dense retrievers may assign high similarity to documents about that concept even when the user asks to avoid it.
We introduce \method, a training-free reranking method for negation-sensitive retrieval.
\method uses an LLM to decompose an exclusionary query into a target intent and a flipped trap intent, then penalizes documents that are similar to the trap.
On ExcluIR, \method reduces violation@5 by up to 97\% across four embedding models, revealing a controllable recall--violation trade-off for exclusion-aware retrieval.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Retrieval is often treated as a relevance problem, but grounded language models need retrieval to satisfy a stronger condition: the returned evidence must be faithful to the user's full intent.
This becomes difficult when intent is expressed through negation or exclusion.
A query such as ``documentaries about the Iraq war, excluding \textit{Gunner Palace}'' asks for documents about the broad topic while ruling out a highly related entity.
Dense retrievers can easily retrieve the excluded entity because it is semantically close to the desired topic.
In a retrieval-augmented generation pipeline, such documents can ground an answer in precisely the evidence the user asked to avoid.

Recent work shows that negation is a persistent weakness for neural retrieval.
NevIR tests whether retrievers can distinguish minimally different document pairs whose relevance flips under negation \citep{weller-etal-2024-nevir}.
ExcluIR extends this issue to realistic exclusionary queries \citep{zhang-etal-2024-excluir}, and NegConstraint studies negative constraints with a neural-symbolic reranker \citep{xu-etal-2025-negconstraint}.
These approaches highlight the importance of negation-aware retrieval, but they often require specialized data, training, or model changes.

We ask whether an existing retriever can be made more faithful to exclusionary intent at inference time.
We propose \method, a simple knowledge-free reranker.
Given an exclusionary query, an LLM produces two short rewrites: a target query that preserves what should be retrieved and a trap query that names the flipped or forbidden intent.
Documents are then rescored by rewarding similarity to the target while subtracting similarity to the trap.
The contribution is deliberately small and practical: \method is a drop-in score transformation for retrievers that expose comparable similarity scores.

Our experiments on ExcluIR show that this simple penalty can greatly reduce constraint violations.
Across four dense retrievers, top-5 violation rates fall from 0.740--0.883 under the baseline to 0.090--0.115 under the best \method settings, with top-5 recall remaining between 0.884 and 0.914.
We also analyze failure cases and find that many errors arise when the answer document itself mentions the forbidden entity, suggesting that negation-sensitive retrieval needs document-level evidence localization rather than only query-level rewriting.

\subsection{Query Decomposition}

Let $q$ be an exclusionary query.
\method asks an LLM to produce two rewrites:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $q_{\mathrm{target}}$: the positive information need, with the exclusion removed but the main topic preserved;
    \item $q_{\mathrm{trap}}$: the forbidden or flipped intent that should be penalized.
\end{itemize}
For example, the query ``location of Friday Harbor Airport without bringing up Dayton International Airport'' is decomposed into target intent ``location of Friday Harbor Airport'' and trap intent ``Dayton International Airport''.
The LLM is not used to score documents or inspect gold labels; it only produces these two strings.

\subsection{Score Transformation}

Let $s(d,q)$ be the retriever similarity score between document $d$ and query $q$.
The baseline ranks documents by $s(d,q)$, or by a cleaned rewrite of the original query when available.
\method instead computes
\begin{equation}
    S(d) = \alpha s(d,q) + \gamma s(d,q_{\mathrm{target}})
           - \beta s(d,q_{\mathrm{trap}}),
    \label{eq:kflip}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$, $\gamma$, and $\beta$ control the original-query, target, and trap terms.
The simplest variant uses only the original query and trap penalty by setting $\gamma=0$.
The main variant uses target-minus-trap scoring by setting $\alpha=0$ and tuning $\gamma,\beta$.
Because all terms are ordinary retriever scores, \method can be applied to any sparse or dense retriever with comparable scores.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Dataset.}
We evaluate on 3,452 exclusionary queries from ExcluIR \citep{zhang-etal-2024-excluir}.
Each example contains an exclusionary query, a gold answer document, and a trap document corresponding to the excluded intent.
We score against the full ExcluIR corpus of 90,406 documents.
Gold document indices are used only for evaluation.

\paragraph{Retrievers.}
We test four embedding models: BGE-M3, Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B, Qwen3-Embedding-4B, and OpenAI text-embedding-3-small.
For each query, we generate $q_{\mathrm{target}}$ and $q_{\mathrm{trap}}$ with GPT-4o mini.
We evaluate a small parameter grid $\gamma,\beta \in \{0.1,0.25,0.5,0.75,1.0\}$ for target-minus-trap scoring and report the best average recall-minus-violation configuration as a characterization of the attainable recall--violation trade-off.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Recall@$k$ measures whether the gold answer document appears in the top $k$.
Violation@$k$ measures whether the trap document appears in the top $k$.
Right-rank measures whether the answer document is ranked above the trap document.
For exclusionary retrieval, higher recall and right-rank are better, while lower violation is better.

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} shows that \method consistently reduces trap retrieval.
The baseline retrievers achieve high recall, but they often retrieve the forbidden document as well: violation@5 ranges from 0.740 to 0.883.
With \method, violation@5 falls below 0.12 for every retriever.
The strongest setting, Qwen3-Embedding-4B, reduces violation@5 from 0.746 to 0.090 while retaining 0.910 recall@5.

The trade-off is clearest for BGE-M3 and text-embedding-3-small.
Both models obtain large violation reductions, but their recall@5 drops by 0.050 and 0.062 respectively.
This is still useful for grounded generation settings where constraint violations are costly, but it shows that a single linear penalty cannot separate all answers from all traps.

\section{Failure Analysis}

We manually inspected failure cases for the target-minus-trap variant.
Two patterns dominate.
First, many gold answer documents mention the excluded entity.
For example, an answer page about a film may list an actor that the query asks to exclude.
Because \method penalizes document-level similarity to the trap query, it can demote otherwise correct documents that contain incidental trap evidence.
Second, many answer and trap documents are near-neighbors in embedding space: they concern sibling entities, adaptations, cast members, or closely related institutions.
In these cases, the trap penalty often lowers both documents together.

These failures suggest that \method is best viewed as a lightweight retrieval-time guardrail, not a complete solution to negation.
Future systems should combine query-level flipped-intent penalties with passage- or span-level evidence checks so that an incidental mention of the forbidden entity does not automatically invalidate an otherwise useful document.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Negation-sensitive retrieval.}
NevIR demonstrates that neural retrievers often fail on paired examples where negation reverses relevance \citep{weller-etal-2024-nevir}.
ExcluIR collects realistic exclusionary queries and shows that retrievers struggle to satisfy what users do not want \citep{zhang-etal-2024-excluir}.
NegConstraint frames negative constraints as a logical consistency problem and uses neural-symbolic reranking \citep{xu-etal-2025-negconstraint}.
\method differs by avoiding training and symbolic parsing: it uses an LLM only to expose a target and trap query, then applies a simple score transformation.

\paragraph{Grounded generation.}
Although our experiments are retrieval-only, the motivation is grounded language generation.
If the evidence set contains documents that violate an exclusionary query, the generator may produce an answer grounded in disallowed evidence.
Negation-sensitive retrieval is therefore a useful front-end guardrail for grounding and faithfulness.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented \method, a knowledge-free flipped-intent penalty for exclusionary retrieval.
By decomposing a query into target and trap intents and subtracting trap similarity, \method substantially reduces retrieval of forbidden documents across four dense retrievers.
The method is simple and training-free, making it attractive for grounded language systems that need fast retrieval-time constraint handling.
Its main limitation is document-level over-penalization when correct documents mention excluded entities, motivating future work on localized evidence checks.

\section*{Limitations}

\method depends on the quality of LLM-generated decompositions and assumes that retriever scores are comparable across target and trap queries.
The reported \method settings are selected from a small parameter search on the evaluated sample to characterize the trade-off; future work should tune hyperparameters on a separate development set.
Our evaluation focuses on ExcluIR and dense retrieval.
Although exclusion is common in natural queries, there are few high-quality benchmarks built specifically for exclusion-aware retrieval, and even fewer provide reliable document-level links for both target and forbidden evidence.
This makes broad benchmark coverage difficult.
At the same time, this scarcity motivates inference-time methods such as \method: because it does not require task-specific training data, it can be applied efficiently to natural exclusion queries with existing retrievers.
Broader tests should include sparse retrieval, cross-encoder reranking, and downstream RAG answer quality.
The current linear penalty may over-demote correct documents that contain incidental mentions of excluded entities.

\section*{Ethics Statement}

This work aims to improve user control over retrieved evidence.
However, an overly aggressive trap penalty can suppress relevant documents, especially when excluded entities are mentioned for context.
Systems using this method should expose uncertainty and avoid treating absence from retrieved evidence as proof that excluded content is absent from the corpus.
